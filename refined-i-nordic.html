How to Sit with a Question You Can't Answer

The useful thought is rarely the one that arrives quickly.

Isaac Lindell
9 min read
Think Pieces

The problem with most thinking is that it wants to arrive somewhere. There is a destination in mind from the start — an answer, a position, a view that can be defended — and the thinking is organised around reaching it. What this produces is not thought. It is the appearance of thought, arranged to confirm what was already suspected.

The useful kind of thinking is the kind that doesn't know where it's going. It is slow, circuitous, and slightly embarrassing — you end up in places you didn't expect, holding positions you're not sure you believe, asking questions that don't have clean answers. Most people, understandably, prefer the other kind.

The question that sits with you is the one that's working on something real. The quick answer is almost always borrowed.

The discipline — if that's the right word — is in staying with the question when you want to resolve it. The mind's instinct is to close. To reach for the nearest available explanation and declare the matter settled. This feels like intelligence. It is usually the opposite.

I have been practicing, with mixed results, the art of the unfinished thought. The piece left open. The conclusion deferred. It is uncomfortable in a way that feels productive — like the discomfort of a good stretch rather than a bad injury. You can usually tell the difference, if you're honest with yourself. The honest part, as always, is the difficult bit.

The Swimming Instructor in October

Every year, at the end of the season, she stayed in the water a little longer.

Isaac Lindell
6 min read
Short Stories

The last lesson of the season was always on a Saturday in late October, when the temperature of the outdoor pool had dropped to the point where the council required it to close. Inger had been teaching there for twenty-two years. She knew, each year, which lesson would be the last before the notice went up on the gate.

She always volunteered to lock up. The caretaker was grateful — he had a long drive home and the locking-up took time, and there was nothing in the pool shed worth stealing. What he didn't know, and what Inger did not tell him, was that she stayed in the water after everyone left.

Not long. Twenty minutes, perhaps thirty. Long enough for the light to change. Long enough to be the only person in the water in October, which is a specific and unrepeatable experience — the cold, the silence, the sensation of occupying a space designed for summer that summer has abandoned.

She was not sure why she did it. She had never tried to explain it, even to herself. It felt like a small act of fidelity to something she couldn't name — the season, perhaps, or the water, or the version of herself who had first learned to swim in a pool not unlike this one, in another October, a long time ago.

She got out when her fingers went. Dried off quickly, the way you do when there's no one watching. Locked the gate. Drove home in wet hair. Every year the same. Every year it felt, somehow, necessary.

Winter Light, Domestic

Isaac Lindell
3 min read
North-facing window. Early February. Someone already awake.
The light comes in at an angle it hasn't used since November. Someone in this house woke early. I can tell by the coffee.
The window holds the street the way a frame holds a painting: insisting it's art, this ordinary thing.
I have been in this room for four winters now. Each time the light returns I am surprised.
I should know by now that it always does. I hope I never do.

The Geography of Everything You Can't Say

There are ideas that have no name yet. They live in the white space between what is known and what can be said.

Isaac Lindell
13 min read
Think Pieces

Before a thing has a name, it exists in a kind of limbo — present, detectable, exerting force on the world around it, but impossible to hand to someone else. You can gesture at it. You can describe its outline the way you might describe a shape in a dark room: "It's about yea high, roughly this shape, cold on one side." But the gestalt of the thing, its essence, remains untransferable until the name arrives.

I have been living with one of these unnamed things for some time now. It is something to do with the specific quality of awareness that comes in the space between two thoughts — not the thoughts themselves, but the gap. The pause. The moment the mind, having finished one thing, has not yet started the next. It feels important. I cannot tell you why.

The map is not the territory. But without the map, the territory has no edges.

Language shapes thought. This is not a new observation. What is less often noted is the inverse: the absence of language shapes thought too. The thing you cannot name, you cannot fully think. You circle it. You approach from different angles. Sometimes you triangulate — if it's not quite this, and not quite that, then it must be somewhere in the middle of this triangle. Sometimes that's enough. Sometimes it isn't.

The best writing, I think, is the kind that names the previously unnamed — not by inventing a word, usually, but by describing the territory so precisely that the reader recognises the map as their own. "Yes," they think, "I have been there. I didn't know anyone else had." That recognition, that relief — that is what the best sentences do. They hand you a map for somewhere you already live.

"The best sentences hand you a map for somewhere you already live."

The Lighthouse Keeper's Register

He logged every ship. One night, he logged one that wasn't there.

Isaac Lindell
7 min read
Short Stories

The register was a large cloth-bound ledger that had been maintained, with occasional gaps, since 1887. Henrik had inherited it from his predecessor as one inherits furniture — not by choice, but by occupancy. He added to it each watch, recording in a hand slightly smaller than the one before him: the time, the vessel name where visible, the direction, the sea state, the sky.

On the night of the fourteenth of February, in the third winter of his tenure, he wrote: 22:14 — vessel, no visible name — bearing NNE — heading SW — sea state moderate — sky clear — note: lights in configuration I do not recognise. Have checked charts. Nothing scheduled. No response to signal.

In the morning he reviewed the entry. He considered crossing it out. He did not. Whatever he had seen was real enough that his hand had recorded it. He had learned, over three winters, to trust his hand.

He mentioned it once, to a colleague who visited in spring. The colleague made the expression people make when they are deciding whether to take something seriously. He took it seriously, for about thirty seconds, then moved on to the difficulty of sourcing decent coffee on the peninsula.

The register was eventually donated to a maritime museum. An archivist, cataloguing it sixty years later, paused at the February entry. She read it three times. She moved on. But she remembered it. She mentioned it once, years later, at a dinner party. Everyone made the expression.

Coordinates

Isaac Lindell
3 min read
Found in the margin of an old atlas.
You can locate anything if you know two numbers. What you cannot locate is why you went there.
The map is patient. It has been here longer than any of the places it claims to contain.
I put my finger here. This is where I am. The map does not care. That's what I like about it.
North is always north. The rest is opinion.

Everything I Know About Failure I Learned at Speed

A meditation on what the racetrack teaches that the classroom never could.

Isaac Lindell
12 min read
Think Pieces

There is a particular kind of failure that is only available at speed. Not the slow catastrophe of the gradual mistake — the career taken wrong, the marriage quietly unloved — but the sudden, total, unambiguous failure that arrives in a fraction of a second and leaves you sitting in the gravel wondering which way is up.

I have had a few of those. Not behind a wheel, I should say immediately — I am not that interesting. My failures have been smaller. A pitch in front of thirty people. A conversation I thought I was winning until I clearly wasn't. The piece I published at twenty-four that I still cannot reread. But the mechanism is the same.

The useful lesson is not "don't fail." The useful lesson is: learn to read the surface before you push.

What the racetrack teaches — or so I am told by people who actually drive on them — is that failure is information. The front wheels losing grip tells you something precise about what you asked them to do. The spin that comes from braking too late tells you where the threshold was. You did not know it was there until you crossed it. Now you do.

The classroom teaches you to avoid failure. Do not raise your hand unless you are certain. Do not submit until it is finished. Do not begin until you understand what you are doing. The racetrack teaches you to fail fast enough, and small enough, that you can still learn from it and drive home.

I am not sure I have fully learned the second lesson. I still have the classroom instinct — the deep reluctance to be wrong in public, the preference for the considered position over the honest guess. But I am working on it. Slowly. Which is, perhaps, the wrong speed for this particular lesson.

The Man Who Collected Silences

A short story about what we keep, and what we call it.

Isaac Lindell
7 min read
Short Stories

He had started, as most collectors do, without intending to. The first silence he kept was the one after his mother's last word to him, which had been his name, spoken from a hospital bed with something he chose to interpret as approval. He held it in his chest for the drive home. He did not play the radio.

The second was the silence of the flat when he returned from her funeral. Not the heavy silence of grief — he had expected that, had braced for it as one braces for cold water. It was the other kind. The silence of a space that had reorganised itself around an absence and found that it fit better this way.

By the third silence he understood that he was doing something deliberate. He began to keep notes. Not of what the silence followed, but of its quality — the way some silences rang slightly, like a glass that had just been set down, and others were flat and final, like felt.

He told no one about this. He was not embarrassed by it, precisely. He simply did not know how to explain a collection that had no form, no location, and no way of being shown to a visitor.

"What do you collect?" a woman asked him once, at a dinner party, the way people ask such questions — looking for the charming answer, the unusual hobby that would make the rest of the table lean in.

He thought for a moment. "Moments after things," he said. She nodded as if this was a normal answer. Perhaps, he thought, it was.

Ode to the Last Train North

Isaac Lindell
3 min read
Poems
Written on a platform in February. The train was late.
You arrive and I am already half gone, coat on, bag at my feet, which is how I love most things — ready to leave before they start.
The platform empties around me. A pigeon walks the yellow line as if it knows the rule does not apply to it.
I have not been north in three years. What I am returning to is not a place but the person I was when I left it.
The train arrives on time. I do not get on. I stand there a moment longer, watching it go.

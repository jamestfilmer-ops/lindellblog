On the Virtue of Going Nowhere in Particular

The explorer who stopped exploring has something the young ones don't.

Isaac Lindell
11 min read
Think Pieces

There is a particular kind of restlessness that masquerades as ambition. It propels people out of rooms, cities, relationships — not because the destination is better but because movement feels like progress, and progress feels like the point. I spent a significant portion of my twenties in the grip of it.

The antidote, it turns out, is not stillness. It is the decision to be interested in exactly where you are. This is harder than it sounds. The mind keeps drifting to where it isn't, tallying what it has not seen, measuring the distance between here and wherever here is not.

The interesting country is always the one that isn't on the map yet. Occasionally, it turns out to be the room you're sitting in.
— Think Pieces

What the experienced traveller knows — the one who has been everywhere and come home — is that the quality of attention matters more than the quality of the view. A person who is genuinely curious about the place they are sitting can find more there than a bored person finds in three continents.

I write this from a desk I have sat at for four years. Outside the window: a street I know by heart, a sky that is doing something unremarkable. I notice, this morning, that the tree at the corner has started something new with its branches. I hadn't seen that before. I had looked, but I hadn't seen.

That might be the whole argument, actually. The difference between looking and seeing is not about where you are. It's about whether you've decided to be there.

"The difference between looking and seeing is not about where you are."

The Postmaster of Tromsø

He'd received letters for thirty years from someone who never existed.

Isaac Lindell
8 min read
Short Stories

The letters began arriving in 1986, addressed in a careful hand to a person whose name did not appear in any register, any church record, any telephone directory. Bjørn — who ran the post office on the hill — set them aside at first, as one does. He left them for a fortnight. Then a month. Then, because nobody claimed them and the name was attached to no known forwarding address, he opened the first one.

It was from a woman in Bergen. She wrote about her garden, about her daughter who was doing well at school, about the length of the summer, which had been kind. She asked after a dog she called Halfdan. She signed it with a name Bjørn had never heard in sixty-two years of living in that town: Solveig.

He wrote back. He was not sure why. He invented a reply — said the dog was well, the summer had been good here too. He did not know what else to do with the letter, and it seemed wrong to let it go unanswered.

Solveig wrote again in October. She was glad to hear from him. She said she thought he had forgotten her. Bjørn wrote back. He said he had not forgotten. He would never forget.

This continued for thirty years. He never met her. She never came north. When she died, her daughter wrote to tell him — this man whose name she did not know, this stranger who had kept her mother company in the long winters through a correspondence that had no explanation and required none.

Bjørn attended the funeral in Bergen. He stood at the back. The daughter saw him and seemed to understand. Nobody asked him anything. He took the train home. He retired the following spring. He kept every letter in a box under the counter, in order, in their envelopes, as if one day they might need to be returned.

Latitude

Isaac Lindell
3 min read
After the atlas. Before sleep.
This far north, the light does not so much set as make its case and leave.
I have been measuring the distance from here to there and finding it grows in the telling.
On the map, this place is a small mark in a large nothing. On the ground, it is the world.
I draw a line with my finger from here to the edge. The edge is further than it looks. It always is.
